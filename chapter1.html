Loving the God We Cannot See Through the People We Can See

Chapter 1: Loving God Became Easier Than Loving People

Miriam’s devotion to Jesus had long been the most stable and unquestioned part of her life. Before the sky hinted at dawn, while the world still slept, she would rise quietly from her bed and kneel on the worn patch of carpet beside it. There, in the stillness, she spoke to Jesus not as a distant deity but as a present and attentive companion. Her prayers were unhurried and familiar, marked by worship, whispered gratitude, and a deep sense of safety. With Him, there was no fear of misinterpretation, no risk of betrayal. Jesus was faithful, constant, and gentle. Loving Him felt effortless.

Yet that same tenderness vanished the moment people entered the picture. Ordinary interactions often stirred irritation she could not explain. A casual comment could feel intrusive. A simple question could awaken suspicion. Over time, Miriam noticed how quickly her heart closed when faced with human connection. Where Jesus drew her inward, people pushed her away.

This was not without reason. Early in life, Miriam had learned that people possessed a unique ability to wound. Friends she trusted had shared her private struggles carelessly. Spiritual leaders she respected had spoken to her with judgment rather than grace. Even those who loved her most had misunderstood her intentions and dismissed her pain. Each disappointment left a mark, and each mark quietly shaped a belief she rarely voiced but deeply held. God was safe. People were not.

Gradually, she began to simplify her faith in a way that felt justified. Loving Jesus, she reasoned, was the purest expression of devotion. Human relationships were optional and at times unnecessary. She attended church faithfully but avoided closeness. She declined invitations with polite excuses. Small groups, shared meals, and meaningful conversations were replaced with solitude and spiritual routines. In her mind, she had chosen holiness over hurt.

One evening, long after the sanctuary had emptied, Miriam remained behind. The lights were dimmed, and the building seemed to exhale into silence. The steady hum of the air conditioning filled the space, accompanied only by the sound of her breathing. She knelt at the altar and began to pray as she always did, expecting the familiar peace to settle over her.

Instead, she felt a weight in her chest. It was not heavy with condemnation, nor sharp with fear. It was tender, pressing, and unmistakably alive. In that moment, the presence of Jesus felt closer than ever, as if He stood beside her, not merely listening but addressing the unspoken places of her heart.

“You love Me because I am safe to love. But I did not save you to hide from My body.”

The words pierced gently yet deeply. Miriam’s composure gave way, and tears came freely. Memories surfaced one after another. She had declined the invitations. The friendships she had avoided repairing. The walls she had built and defended in the name of self preservation. She saw, perhaps for the first time, how her devotion had become a refuge that also functioned as a barrier. Her love for God, though sincere, had been shaped by fear rather than trust.

That night, her prayer changed. She did not rehearse familiar phrases or recite well worn petitions. She confessed honestly. She named her fear. She acknowledged the years she had guarded herself from pain by guarding herself from people. In the quiet of the sanctuary, she released what she had carried for decades, placing it into the hands of Jesus with trembling faith.

The following week brought an unexpected test. As she walked through the church hallway, a fellow member approached her. This was someone who had hurt her deeply years before, a wound she had never addressed, only buried. Her instinct was to withdraw, to offer a brief nod and move on. Instead, she paused. She met the person’s eyes. She smiled and spoke a simple greeting.

There was no rehearsed forgiveness, no forced conversation. Just recognition. Just openness.

As they parted, Miriam noticed something unfamiliar. Her chest felt lighter, her steps less burdened. It was as if something unseen had shifted within her. She understood then that this was not the result of personal effort or emotional discipline. It was the work of the Spirit of Jesus, quietly reshaping her heart.

Miriam realized that loving Jesus could never be separated from loving the people He created and redeemed. Her faith was not meant to exist in isolation but in communion. And for the first time in a long while, she felt free.
