Loving the God We Cannot See Through the People We Can See

Chapter 2: When Church Hurt Teaches the Wrong Lessons

Samuel’s earliest memories were inseparable from the life of the church. His parents were pastors, and ministry was not something that happened around the edges of family life. It was the framework through which everything else was filtered. His childhood unfolded in sanctuaries and fellowship halls, between rehearsals and services, sermons and altar calls. Scripture was memorized as easily as school assignments. Worship songs were learned before playground rhymes. He knew how to stand, how to speak, how to serve, and how to appear spiritually mature long before he understood what any of it truly meant.

From the outside, Samuel looked like the ideal pastor’s kid. He was present at every event, helpful without complaint, and fluent in the language of faith. Yet beneath that polished surface, a quiet confusion began to take root. The Jesus he heard preached from the pulpit spoke of mercy, patience, and grace. The Jesus he encountered through people often felt harsh and unpredictable. Leaders who declared forgiveness as central to the gospel mocked small mistakes behind closed doors. Teachers who demanded holiness left no room for growth or repentance. Failure was remembered long after apologies were offered, and weakness was treated as a flaw rather than a place for healing.

As Samuel entered his teenage years, he learned an unspoken lesson. Survival required concealment. He discovered that it was safer to perform than to be honest, safer to comply than to question. His faith in Jesus remained intact, even strong, but his trust in people eroded steadily. He learned how to guard his emotions, how to keep his thoughts private, and how to smile convincingly while remaining inwardly distant. The pain he experienced did not drive him away from God. Instead, it taught him to separate God from those who claimed to represent Him.

By the time Samuel reached adulthood, this separation had become a way of life. His personal devotion was disciplined and sincere. He prayed regularly, studied Scripture diligently, and maintained a deep respect for the name of Jesus. Yet his relationships were marked by distance. Friendships rarely moved beyond surface level conversations. Accountability felt threatening rather than supportive. Emotional intimacy was avoided, not because he lacked desire, but because vulnerability felt unsafe. The wounds he carried from church life had quietly become walls, defining the limits of his connection with others.

Everything shifted one evening at a small worship gathering held in a friend’s home. The setting was informal, far removed from the structure and expectations of his upbringing. As voices lifted in song, Samuel felt something unfamiliar stir within him. A warmth spread through his chest, growing more intense by the moment. His hands began to tremble, not from fear, but from a sensation he could not control. Without warning, memories surfaced with startling clarity. Moments of public correction. Words spoken carelessly by authority figures. The humiliation he had buried for years rose to the surface of his mind.

In the midst of this overwhelming experience, the Spirit of Jesus spoke with quiet authority to his heart. The message was simple and devastatingly clear. “You separated Me from My body to protect yourself.”

Samuel’s instinct was resistance. He tried to steady himself, to regain composure, to suppress what was happening. But the more he resisted, the stronger the presence became. Finally, exhausted by years of restraint, he surrendered. He bowed his head and allowed the Spirit to move freely through the places he had long guarded. There were no dramatic words, no loud declarations. Instead, something far more difficult took place. Samuel began to pray for the people he had avoided for years. Not with the goal of reconciliation, and not to excuse their behavior, but to release the bitterness that had anchored itself in his heart.

As he prayed, the trembling slowly subsided. In its place came a peace that did not rely on explanation or logic. It was deep, steady, and unmistakably real. Samuel knew that something supernatural had occurred. The presence of Jesus had not dismissed his pain, but it had begun to heal it.

In the weeks that followed, his life began to change in small but significant ways. He cautiously stepped back into community, choosing participation over isolation. He attended small groups, even when discomfort lingered. He initiated conversations with people he once kept at a distance. In moments that required courage, he reached out to mentors who had previously wounded him, not demanding resolution, but allowing space for growth and understanding.

The evidence of transformation became impossible to ignore. Patterns that had governed his relationships for decades began to loosen their grip. Fear no longer dictated his boundaries. Trust, though rebuilt slowly, felt possible again. Samuel came to understand that obedience to the Spirit of Jesus was not about denying past hurt, but about refusing to let that hurt define his future. And as healing unfolded, he discovered that the body of Christ, once a source of pain, could also become a place of restoration.
